Line-Following Robot

My first robot that actually does something on its own: an Arduino reads a row of infrared sensors and steers two motors to keep a black line underneath it.

The idea

I wanted a first robot that did something visibly clever but wasn't over my head. A line follower is perfect: it senses the world (where the line is), makes a decision (turn left, turn right, go straight), and acts (drives the motors). That's the whole sense–decide–act loop in one cheap little machine.

// goal: build it from parts I understand, not a kit — so I can fix it when it misbehaves (it did).

Building the chassis

The base is a laser-cut acrylic plate with two yellow TT gear motors at the back and a single caster wheel up front. Getting the motors mounted square mattered more than I expected — a couple of degrees off and the robot drifts even on a straight line. The sensor bar hangs off the front, about 8 mm above the floor.

Reading the line

Each IR sensor shines light down and measures how much bounces back — black line reflects little, white floor reflects a lot. With five sensors in a row I can tell not just that the robot is off the line but which way. I read them as a simple position value: negative means the line drifted left, positive means right.

diagram — sensor readings to steering direction
FIG 3 — how the five sensor values map to a turn

Making it steer

My first version was “bang-bang”: if the line is left, turn hard left; if right, turn hard right. It worked but wobbled like crazy. The fix was to make the turn proportional — a small error gives a gentle correction, a big error a sharp one. That one change turned a drunken zig-zag into a smooth curve. It's the same idea behind PID, just the easy first piece of it.

What I'd change next

It loses the line on tight corners if I push the speed up, so the next version gets faster sensors and I'll finally add the other two PID terms. I'd also move the messy breadboard wiring onto the little shield I designed (PROJ-05) so it stops falling apart in my bag.
