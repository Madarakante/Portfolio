I use a €12 multimeter at home and a much nicer bench meter in the lab. For learning, the cheap one is genuinely fine — with one caveat.

## What the cheap meter nails

Voltage, resistance and continuity — the three things you'll use 95% of the time — are all accurate enough on a budget meter. The continuity beep alone has saved me from countless wiring mistakes. For checking a battery, finding a short, or confirming a resistor value, it does everything I need.

## Where it falls short

Two things. The cheap meter is **slow to settle** and its **current ranges are fiddly and easy to blow the fuse on**. The bench meter measures faster, auto-ranges cleanly, and feels sturdier — but none of that changes whether your circuit works.

<div class="pull-note">// <b>the one upgrade worth it</b> — a meter with a proper continuity beep that responds *instantly*. A laggy beep makes probing a dense board miserable. Spend a little here, save elsewhere.</div>

## Verdict

As a student, buy the cheap one, learn on it, and use the lab's bench meter when you need precision. You'll know when you've outgrown it — and by then you'll know exactly which features you actually want.
